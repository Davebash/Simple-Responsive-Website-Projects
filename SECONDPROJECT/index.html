Dr. Jonas Salk

The man who saved a billion lives

Here's a time line of Dr. Salk's life:

  • 1914 - Born in New York City
  • 1934 - Enrolls in the College of the City of New York as a pre-med student
  • 1939 - Graduates from NYU School of Medicine, where he originally wanted to be a lawyer but switched to medicine
  • 1942 - Begins working on flu vaccines at the University of Michigan's School of Public Health under Dr. Thomas Francis
  • 1947 - Becomes head of the Virus Research Laboratory at the University of Pittsburgh
  • 1952 - Conducts the first successful human trials of the polio vaccine
  • 1954 - The vaccine undergoes a large-scale field trial involving over 1.8 million children, the largest in history at the time
  • 1955 - Salk's polio vaccine is declared "safe and effective," virtually eradicating polio in the US
  • 1955 - Refuses to patent the vaccine or profit from it, famously stating, "There is no patent. Could you patent the sun?"
  • 1960 - Founds the Salk Institute for Biological Studies in La Jolla, California, focusing on medical research and biological science
  • 1970s - Works on research into multiple sclerosis and AIDS vaccines
  • 1983 - Begins researching a potential AIDS vaccine
  • 1995 - Dies at the age of 80 in La Jolla, California

"Dr. Salk's relentless pursuit of medical research gave the world not only a cure for polio but hope for the eradication of other diseases. His legacy endures in every life saved."

— Former President Bill Clinton
